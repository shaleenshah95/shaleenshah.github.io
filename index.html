Nursing Home Inspect

Mapping nursing homes with serious deficiencies and the consequences. The materials from this project are from ProPublica's original story, Nursing Home Inspect.

By Shaleen Shah

Published September 15, 2019

Nursing homes have been cited for deficiencies by regulators. This has resulted in the nursing homes either being fined or their incoming payments being suspended.

The number of serious deficiencies per nursing home differs by state, so does the average fine and payment suspensions. However, the three values don’t always correlate with each other.

This map shows the number of serious deficiencies per home in each state. The homes are Medicare certified. The level of deficiency of a nursing home is rated on a scale of A to L. Homes rates as J, K or L are defined as homes having serious deficiencies. The values on the map are calculated by the formula: the number of serious deficiencies in each state divided by the current number of Medicare certified homes in the state.

The average fine is calculated as the total fines paid by a nursing home divided by the number of fines. It’s important to remember that this is calculated for the past three years. Interestingly, this map doesn’t correspond with the previous one, likely because urban areas are more expensive than rural ones. Even though states like New Mexico have higher serious deficiencies per home, they are fined less, on average, than urban states in the East coast.

One way the nursing homes were punished for having serious deficiencies is by having their payments suspended from new admissions. This map shows how many times homes were penalized in the past three years by having their payments suspended. Contrary to Map 2, this one corresponds to Map 1, which shows the serious deficiencies per home. For example, Texas, which has a high number of serious deficiencies per home, also has a high number of times homes were penalized through suspension of payments for new admissions.
